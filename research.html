Research Programme

Key Research Areas

Seven interconnected domains spanning energy materials, semiconductor physics, ion-beam science, 2D materials, and space instrumentation — unified by experimental precision and indigenous instrumentation.

01
Thermoelectric Materials & Energy Harvesting

Converting Waste Heat into Usable Power

Thermoelectric devices offer a direct pathway for converting temperature gradients into electricity without moving parts — ideal for waste-heat recovery, remote power generation, and flexible wearable electronics. This research programme investigates both inorganic wide-bandgap thermoelectrics (GaN, β-Ga₂O₃, SiC) operating at elevated temperatures, and organic-inorganic hybrid composites (PEDOT:PSS/Bi₂Te₃/rGO) as printable, flexible energy harvesters.

Key milestones include the first comprehensive thermoelectric characterisation of β-Ga₂O₃ single crystals across 80–630 K, and the most complete combined experimental and DFT study of GaN as a thermoelectric material. The group has demonstrated that swift heavy ion irradiation can precision-engineer thermopower in GaN — a research direction that opened an entirely new international niche.

02
Defects in Wide-Bandgap Semiconductors

Deep-Level Physics in Next-Generation Materials

Wide-bandgap semiconductors (GaN, SiC, β-Ga₂O₃, AlN, ZnO) are the backbone of modern power electronics and high-frequency devices. Their performance is determined critically by native and radiation-induced deep-level defects. This programme uses Deep Level Transient Spectroscopy (DLTS), 1/f noise analysis, Hall-effect transport, and in-situ beamline characterisation to map defect landscapes in these materials across cryogenic to high temperatures (80 K – 500 K).

A defining achievement is the unique in-situ DLTS measurement capability of GaN Schottky diodes under live swift heavy ion beam irradiation — capturing defect formation and annealing kinetics in real time, impossible to observe through post-irradiation measurements alone.

03
III-V / IV Semiconductor Devices

Radiation-Hard Devices for Defence & Space

GaN/AlGaN HEMT structures, SiC Schottky contacts, and GaN-based Schottky barrier diodes are critical for next-generation radar, satellite communications, and power conversion. This programme studies their electrical transport, barrier physics, surface passivation strategies (ruthenium-based passivation — a decade-long body of work published in APL 2014 and APL 2023), and radiation hardness under gamma, proton, and heavy-ion exposure.

The active DRDO CARS project extends this work to radiation hardness requirements for defence-grade semiconductor components, while the ongoing IUAC project investigates ion-beam modification as a precision tool for device engineering.

04
Ion–Matter Interactions

Engineering Materials with Swift Heavy Ions

Swift heavy ions (SHI) — typically hundreds of MeV heavy ions from particle accelerators — deposit energy into solids via electronic excitation, creating cylindrical latent damage tracks. This programme exploits SHI irradiation as a precision tool for modifying electrical, optical, and thermoelectric properties of semiconductor materials.

All experimental work is conducted at IUAC beamlines (New Delhi), where unique in-situ capabilities — developed by the PI — allow real-time electrical measurement during live beam irradiation. Collaborations with TEM groups at HZDR, Germany enable direct correlation of electrical modification with atomic-scale structural changes.

05
2D Materials

Graphene, MoS₂ & Carbon Nanostructures

Two-dimensional materials offer extraordinary electrical, thermal, and optical properties suitable for ultrathin thermoelectric generators, photodetectors, and novel Schottky junctions. This programme investigates graphene modification via swift heavy ion irradiation, MoS₂-based Schottky junctions with controlled barrier heights, and the role of graphene derivatives (rGO) in enhancing composite thermoelectric performance.

06
EUV / X-ray Space Exploration

Nanomaterial Mirrors for Polarimetric Space Missions

This programme — supported by ISRO RESPOND (₹60 Lakh) — develops nanomaterial-based multilayer mirror prototypes for extreme ultraviolet (EUV) and soft X-ray polarimetric space missions. The work combines thin film deposition, nanoscale characterisation, and optical modeling to design mirrors with precisely engineered reflectivity and polarimetric efficiency.

This is an exciting new direction that leverages the group's expertise in thin film physics and materials characterisation to address a direct need of the Indian space programme.

07
Precision Cryogenic Instrumentation

Building the Instruments that Build the Science

A defining and internationally distinctive aspect of this research programme is the indigenous design and commissioning of five custom measurement systems — enabling a fully self-contained experimental capability without dependence on commercial or shared infrastructure. These systems operate across 80 K – 1000 K and collectively enable thermoelectric, defect, noise, and optical characterisation.

The Seebeck coefficient and resistivity system (80 K – 650 K, published in Rev. Sci. Instrum. 2019) is one of very few designed indigenously in India and is now used by multiple national collaborators. The in-situ DLTS system, developed for IUAC beamline use, represents a unique experimental capability.
